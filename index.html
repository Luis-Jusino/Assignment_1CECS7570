Social engineering

Social engineering is when one person tricks another person into sharing confidential information, for example, by posing as someone authorized to have access to that information. Social engineering can take many forms. Any one-to-one communication medium can be used to perform social engineering attacks.(Harris & Maymí, 2016). It is a technique for breaching a system’s security by exploiting human nature rather than technology.(Easttom, 2016)
There's a lot of types of social engineering attacks, here are some examples:
  • Phishing- Attacker sends a fraudulent email posing as a trustworthy entity to trick individuals into revealing sensitive information. This is commonly carried out by a crafted email with a link expected to be clicked for it and the information.
  • Pretexting- Attacker creates believable scenario in an effort to persuade the target to get sensitive information or to impersonate some of in authority and get information fraudulently.
  • Baiting- The attacker offers something enticing to lure individuals into taking actions that compromise security.
  • Vishing- Also called voice phishing, using phone calls to deceive individuals into providing sensitive information.
  • Tailgating or Piggybacking- Physically following someone with authorized access into a secured area without authentication.

Access Control

This is a security measure that regulates and restricts access to physical and digital resources. The least privilege the individual can have to do his work effectively the better it is. This is crucial for the CIA triad (Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability). Access control has some key components such as authentication, authorization, access policies, access control lists (ACLs), physical access controls, and encryption. All of these components provide a secure access control system.
